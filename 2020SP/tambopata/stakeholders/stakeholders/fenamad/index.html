Tambopata

Leader of the Federation of Native Peoples (FENAMAD)

Photo credit: John Poole, NPR

Agustin Achuni leads FENAMAD, a grassroots political organization of indigenous peoples. FENAMAD’s mission is to defend indigenous ancestral lands from outside interests and gain authority to manage their legally recognized territories as they choose. Many indigenous communities in this region of the Amazon lost large tracts of their original ancestral lands due to Peru’s land reform policies in the 1970s.

Some communities who are now members of FENAMAD, like Ese’eja The largest group of indigenous people in the region, numbering ~600 today, some estimate ~10,000 before 1890. Ese'eja Community Member and Harakmbut communities, managed to win legal recognition of smaller parts of their ancestral territories. These communities now have legal communal titles but have lost access to many of their sacred sites and to the areas in which they relied on for traditional hunting, fishing, and gathering.

As leader of FENAMAD, Agustin is deeply frustrated by the political marginalization of indigenous peoples, and his strongest priority is to regain indigenous control of ancestral lands. He believes that the entire Tambopata National Reserve and Bahuaja-Sonene Park belong to the Ese’eja, the Harakmbut, and other ethnic groups in the region. He also thinks that Peru’s Park Service should co-manage these protected areas in partnership with the Ese’eja, Harakmbut, and other native groups.

Agustin is also helping FENAMAD members devise ways in which their communities can be sustainable over the long term. Some FENAMAD members believe ecotourism A form of nature-based tourism in which tourist impacts on the environment are mitigated. Rainforest Expeditions can be a profitable business for indigenous communities and a source of jobs for individuals. Others, however, see ecotourism as an activity that commercializes their traditional culture and inequitably distributes the work and the financial benefits involved. Plus, some critics argue that tourism and wildlife hunting are simply not compatible.

Similarly, FENAMAD members are divided in their opinion of forest carbon projects – like those referred to as REDD+ "Reduced Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation", is an incentive-based mechanism for reducing greenhouse gas emissions through improved forest management in developing countries. However, other FENAMAD members strongly oppose forest carbon projects, arguing that yet again indigenous communities have had little say in how these REDD+ projects are designed and managed.

Gold mining has brought about another tension within FENAMAD’s constituency. Many FENAMAD members now work as gold miners. Harakmbut people are intensively mining for gold within their Kotsimba Territory and keeping all other miners out. Meanwhile, the Ese’eja people living in the Infierno Territory do not permit any mining on their lands, but they extract gold from neighboring regions. Agustin is pained by his people’s poverty and thinks that if mining must happen, it should directly benefit indigenous peoples. Agustin is also under heavy pressure from some FENAMAD members to help them stop illegal logging by commercial operators on native lands. He is still grieving and furious about the fact that illegal loggers murdered indigenous leaders who were trying to defend their forests in other parts of the Peruvian Amazon.
