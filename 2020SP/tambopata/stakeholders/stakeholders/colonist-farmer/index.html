Tambopata

Colonist Farmer

Photo credit: Yoly Gutierrez, CIFOR

Luisa Herrera and her family moved five years ago to the Tambopata National Reserve in search of new farm land and opportunities. They left Cuzco, a city in the Peruvian Andes, due to land scarcity and unemployment. Immigrants to the Amazon, like Luisa and her family, are often referred to as ‘colonists’. Like other colonists, upon her arrival, Luisa claimed 30 hectares A 100 x 100 m unit of land (1 hectare = 2.3 acres). Abbreviated “ha". Most Tambopata farms are ~40 ha. of land along the highway. Every year, Luisa and her family plant rice and corn in fields cleared in the forest until the soil becomes ‘tired’ and less productive. Then, they convert the old field to cattle pasture and clear a new field in the forest. They eat most of the food they grow and sell the surplus if they have any.

Like other poor residents of Tambopata, Luisa is worried about her family’s financial future. She hopes someday to buy a truck and sell food in town or to local miners. Or, she might convert her whole farm into a pasture and get a job in the city. SHe might even be able to start growing organic cacao (the source of chocolate) and sell it via a local marketing coop that could negotiate a better price. Consumer demand for certified chocolate is on the rise. But Luisa has no knowledge or experience with tropical tree crops like cacao – she would need training to pursue agroforestry. She also wonders how he could survive financially while waiting the five years for the cacao trees to bear fruit. She could always turn to working for her friends in their illegal gold mining camp. Gold mining is back-breaking, often dangerous work, but if you’re lucky, you can make a lot of money in a short time.

Luisa and most other FADEMAD members are poor, but they are the largest interest group in the region, and they have strength in numbers. Luisa and her fellow colonists keep pushing their leaders to take a firm pro-agriculture stand. They know they need financial, technical, and political support to be successful farmers in Tambopata.
