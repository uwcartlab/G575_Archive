Leader of Agrarian Federation (FADEMAD)

Photo credit: John Poole, NPR

Alan Moreno leads FADEMAD, a grassroots political organization that represents ~5000 small farmers in the Madre de Dios Madre de Dios: (literally “Mother of God”) the name of the 8.5 million ha Department (region) surrounding Tambopata. It is the least populated area in Peru (~ 1 person/km2). region. FADEMAD’s goal is to improve the quality of life of its members, both long-time residents and more-recently settled colonists.

Alan believes that poor farmers’ quality of life depends on a healthy environment. Thus, he tries to defend local farmers AND the forest. He grew up in the Amazon, as did his father and grandfather. He’s proud of his farm. He’s planted a wide variety of crops together in a way that protects the soil, includes trees, and attracts wildlife. He thinks this form of agriculture (called agroforestry) is the best hope for protecting the environment while also providing local citizens food and income. As the leader of FADEMAD, he has worked closely with conservationists to help farmers learn about agroforestry.

He’s also intrigued by new opportunities for local poor farmers to earn extra income by selling certain crops–especially cacao–under a certification system. Growing cacao can be a lot of work for very little money, but if cacao could be sold under a certification scheme, this could create a sustainable source of income for Tambopata’s farmers.

To save his political career, Alan recently tried to display a more pro-agriculture stance and publicly announced that local farmers’ rights must take priority over biodiversity.

He is troubled by reports that some miners are threatening local farmers with violence, demanding access to gold lying beneath their lands.

Alan and his fellow FADEMAD members are wary of forest carbon projects–like those referred to as REDD+ Reduced Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation, is an incentive-based mechanism for reducing greenhouse gas emissions through improved forest management in developing countries. (Reduced Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation)– that promise to pay farmers not to clear forest on their land for many years. He questions whether the extra money will be enough for farmers to tie up parts of their land in lengthy conservation contracts. After all, what does a poor farmer have to offer her growing children but her land? However, Alan is interested in carbon projects that pay farmers to adopt agroforestry, including cacao agroforestry systems.

Last, although Alan believes ecotourism A form of nature-based tourism in which tourist impacts on the environment are mitigated. mainly benefits private companies and some Ese’eja indigenous communities, The largest group of indigenous people in the region, numbering ~600 today, some estimate ~10,000 before 1890. he does see promise in ‘agro-tourism’. Some Brazil nut harvesters hope to make money charging tourists for the experience to harvest Brazil nuts.
