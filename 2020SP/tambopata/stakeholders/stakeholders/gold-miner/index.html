Small-Scale Gold Miner

Photo credit: John Poole, NPR

Elena Gomez is a long-time resident of Tambopata and a gold miner who has been mining the banks of the Malinowski river for 20 years. Elena first turned to gold mining when her family was in an economic crisis and she desperately needed to make some cash fast. Like many of her mining friends, she calls himself an 'artisanal' Artisanal mining: small-scale mining preformed by individuals, often on an informal/unofficial basis. technology used has lower impact than industrial mining. miner because she uses only modest equipment. She does not have a formal (legal) mining title or permit. Elena submitted the initial paperwork for a permit to Peru’s Ministry of Energy and Mines, but she can’t afford to pay for the environmental impact assessment a legal permit would require.

As a result, Elena, like the vast majority of the miners she knows, works at the margins of the law. Since Elena has been operating illegally, the military has destroyed her mining equipment twice as part of their crackdown on illegal mining in the region. Now she must work for another miner who buys bigger equipment with the aid of crime syndicates. She is alarmed by increasing violence in mining areas and sees that other old-timer artisanal miners are arming themselves and taking turns at guard when they are at the riverbanks.

Mining has grown in Tambopata in recent years, and larger-scale miners have begun to push artisanal miners as well as farmers off their land with threats of violence. Given the rising global demand for gold, Elena thinks it is impossible to stop mining in Tambopata and that the government should focus on regulating the activity and helping local residents benefit from the activity instead of outsiders. Elena is disturbed by news reports that mining is contaminating the region’s water and fish, yet she is proud to be a miner. She just wishes she could work independently, with no fear of violence, and that she could afford to use lower polluting mining technology.
